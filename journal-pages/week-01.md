# Week 01 - Data Drawings

[← Back to Home](../index.md)

## Overview

In the first week, I learned that data is both a practical tool and a creative medium. This course positions data visualization not merely as a means of clearly conveying information, but as a way to reconnect abstract concepts with everyday life. One of the core concepts that left the deepest impression on me was data humanism, which posits that working with data involves transforming abstract concepts into visual, tangible, and relatable forms. This perspective transformed my first experiment from simply creating charts into designing a visual language rooted in human experience.

The main activity of this week’s workshop was **Group Data Portraits**. For this task, we worked in small groups to design a short questionnaire. After the responses were submitted anonymously, we transformed them into a collective artwork. We then exchanged artwork with another group and interpreted each other’s pieces by identifying patterns, unexpected discoveries, and underlying meanings. This activity helped us realize that data can be collected, interpreted, and presented in many ways, and that the choices made during the visualization process shape different meanings.

## Step 1: Collecting Data

Our group first discussed which everyday data points were worth comparing. We decided to focus on simple, personal questions—questions that, while straightforward, revealed clear differences among group members. The topics covered included screen time, number of siblings, time spent on design projects, time spent on homework, and eating habits. I found this phase particularly meaningful because even the most mundane aspects of daily life can become a dataset once they are organized and recorded.

What I find interesting is that these questions aren’t purely factual in a scientific sense. They are subtle glimpses into people’s daily habits and behaviors. This makes the dataset feel more human and subjective. It also resonates with the classroom discussions about the digitization of everyday life. Behaviors such as movement, attention, and daily habits can all be converted into quantifiable data.

## Step 2: Creating a Collective Data Drawing

After gathering everyone’s feedback, we collaborated to design a visual language for this data. Rather than using standard chart formats, we transformed the answers into shapes, colors, symbols, and page layouts. The resulting artwork is both abstract and playful, using repeated markers and diverse visual forms to suggest connections between the answers.

This part of the workshop was both the most challenging and the most fascinating for me. It was challenging because there were no set templates to follow. We had to decide for ourselves how to translate a number, category, or response into a visual element. At the same time, this freedom made the entire process more creative. I began to think more deeply about how visual decisions affect readability. While a shape can be expressive, if the logic isn’t clear enough, it can make it difficult for others to interpret. This made me realize that data visualization is not just about collecting data, but about striking a balance between representation and communication.

## Step 3: Decoding Another Group’s Drawing

The next activity involved exchanging work with another group and attempting to interpret their data visualizations. From their diagrams, we could see that the data they had collected pertained to topics such as modes of transportation to campus, age, concentration levels, sleep duration, and the number of classes attended that day. We tried to decipher the recurring symbols and numbers to deduce the meaning behind each element. Some parts were relatively easy to understand because the patterns were distinctive, but others remained somewhat ambiguous.

This decoding phase was extremely helpful because it revealed the strengths and weaknesses of the hand-drawn visual system. We learned several things about the group from it, such as the frequent appearance of bus travel, while the members’ sleep patterns seemed to vary. We also noticed some amusing or surprising details, particularly when the drawings suggested that someone might be getting very little sleep. At the same time, not every detail was easy to interpret; in fact, no one was able to successfully decode the yellow sections. This indicates that the success of data visualization depends not only on creativity but also on its ability to effectively assist others in interpreting the information.

## Reflection on In-Class Activity

This experiment changed my perspective on data visualization. Before this, I typically associated data with numbers, charts, and digital tools. However, this activity made me realize that data can stem from conversations, observations, and everyday experiences. It can be tangible, hand-drawn, and open to interpretation.

I also learned that, with thoughtful design, even very small datasets can produce interesting visual effects. Although our questionnaire was simple, the process of transforming it into symbols and patterns brought the information to life. This is closely linked to the concept of data humanism discussed in class: data should not be viewed as a cold and detached entity, but rather as something deeply connected to life experiences, behaviors, and identity. Far from stripping the data of its emotional resonance, the process of visualizing it actually made these human qualities more clearly visible.

Another key insight from this project is the balance between creativity and clarity. While I prefer to create a visual language rather than rely on traditional presentation formats, the decoding process made me realize that communication remains paramount. If the visuals are too abstract, the audience may struggle to grasp the underlying data. In future experiments, I want to explore how to make the structure easier to understand while maintaining the visual appeal of the visualizations.

## Independent Study – Data Portrait

For my independent research project, I created a hand-drawn data portrait based on the color of every food item I ate over the course of a week. I chose this theme because eating is a part of daily life that often seems mundane and unthinking, but I suspected that if I recorded it carefully, it might reveal patterns I had not previously noticed. Furthermore, food itself is highly visually expressive, so I believed color would be an excellent starting point for transforming personal behavior into a painting.

The data collection process was done manually, rather than through an app or digital tool. Over the course of seven days, I recorded the colors that appeared in my meals and categorized them by breakfast, lunch, and dinner. This made me pay closer attention to the actual food I was eating. I no longer viewed food solely in terms of taste or habit; instead, I began to see each meal as a combination of colors. Even a simple meal became a mini-dataset. This process made my daily diet feel more intentional and worth observing. The completed log sheet shows how I tracked recurring colors—such as red, yellow, green, white, brown, orange, purple, and black—over the course of several days.

### Process

When designing the final data portrait, I developed a personal visual system to organise the information. Different background textures represented the days of the week, with each day assigned its own pattern, such as horizontal stripes, vertical stripes, dots, crosshatching, wavy lines, or plain white. I also used shapes to distinguish meals: circles for breakfast, squares for lunch, and triangles for dinner. Colour itself remained the main data layer, since the aim of the project was to represent the visual qualities of food rather than the food items by name. This system allowed me to present a relatively large amount of data in a way that remained hand-made and visually expressive.

## Reflection on Independent Study

I chose to track the colours of every piece of food I ate for a week. I was drawn to this topic because it felt both deeply personal and surprisingly objective. I was curious whether my food choices, when stripped of names and categories, would reveal patterns I was otherwise blind to. Would my meals be monotonous or varied? Would certain days look brighter than others?

The process of collecting data turned out to be surprisingly delightful. A simple lunch turned into a set of data points: the brown of the toast, the green of the lettuce, and the red of the tomato. This transformation turned a meal into a palette of colors and made me more aware of my dietary choices at the moment. Visualizing this data afterward, however, presented a whole new challenge. I had to design a system that could accommodate 63 distinct color entries without becoming chaotic.

Looking at the finished chart, I immediately noticed details I might otherwise have overlooked. For example, I discovered that yellow and green appeared far more frequently than I had expected, while purple was a rare guest, typically showing up only once a day. I also noticed that some meals were extremely vibrant (such as Monday’s breakfast, which featured red, yellow, and white), while others were more subdued. These data points painted a whole new self-portrait: my diet is far more colorful—and perhaps healthier—than I had realized. 

My approach to data collection has been carefully considered. I focus solely on color, which means ignoring everything else: the type of food, the portion size, the taste, who I’m eating with, and the location. This method highlights the purely visual and aesthetic dimensions of my diet. What is left out, however, is the entire context and significance of the meal.

This exercise connects directly to the ideas of data humanism and the Dear Data project. Like Giorgia Lupi and Stefanie Posavec, I moved away from cold, machine-like charts and created a hand-drawn, personal visual language. The legend on the back is the key to my personal code, making the drawing intimate and expressive. It is a humanistic approach because it acknowledges that data is not just numbers, but a record of a life lived. 

## Link Between Class Activity and Independent Study

Reflecting on the first week as a whole, I noticed a clear connection between the data portraits created in class groups and those developed during my independent study. In class, I learned that datasets can originate from subtle personal questions and, through collaboration, be transformed into a collective visual language. In my independent study, I extended this concept into a more personal and sustained form of observation. The classroom activities introduced basic methods for collecting, visualizing, and interpreting data, while my independent study gave me the time to construct a more carefully considered system of presentation. Together, these two activities helped me realize that data-driven design is not merely about clearly presenting information, but also about what to focus on, what to encode, and how to convey life experiences through form.

## Working in Markdown

Another important part of Week 1 was learning that the Making Journal would be built in Markdown as a static website. At first, this felt quite technical, but I now understand why the course uses this format. Markdown treats writing as plain text, which means the journal is lightweight, portable, and easy to reuse in other forms such as a website or PDF. This also aligns with the course’s philosophy of treating the process itself as data. In this sense, the way I document my experiments becomes an integral part of the design practice.

## Next Steps

In the next phase of this experiment, I hope to improve the way I document the production process and the logic behind the final visualizations. If I undertake a similar project again, I plan to test clearer legends or more structured mapping systems so that other teams can interpret these charts more easily. Additionally, I hope to continue exploring ways to present personal and everyday data in visually engaging ways.
