## Reflective Proposal

Reflecting on my Making Journal experiment, I have become increasingly interested in how data shapes people’s understanding of their everyday and environmental experiences. The hand-drawn data collection and personal observations of the first week made me realise that data is never entirely neutral, as even the simplest acts of recording, counting, and categorising already involve interpretation and selection. During the second week, when transforming data into interactive formats, I began to pay closer attention to how audiences navigate information and how interface design influences the formation of meaning. During the third week, whilst working with real-time earthquake data, I gained a deeper appreciation of the fact that ‘data can not only record fixed facts, but also represent continuously changing environmental processes’. By the fourth week, my exploration of the DOC Campsites dataset using AI tools made me realise that data visualisation is not merely about presenting information, but rather about deciding what story to tell and for whom that story is intended. For me, DOC Campsites is currently the most meaningful direction to pursue, as it connects public environmental information, tourism, infrastructure, and people’s everyday relationship with New Zealand’s natural spaces.

The project theme I wish to explore in depth is how local environmental data is increasingly shaping the way people understand New Zealand’s natural environment. The future scenario I wish to explore is one in which the public’s understanding of the natural environment derives less and less from local memory or long-term lived experience, and increasingly from the classification, filtering and organisation of natural spaces by digital systems. This future scenario is not necessarily limited to camping itself, but rather reflects a broader state of affairs: whether local residents, tourists or communities, people are gradually coming to understand forests, coastlines, conservation areas and rural locations through data platforms, visual interfaces and information systems. In such a scenario, experiences of nature will be reorganised according to data categories such as access, capacity, facilities, restrictions and regional identity. My project will primarily utilise the DOC Campsites dataset from data.govt.nz. As this is existing public data, it is static rather than real-time; however, it is sufficiently comprehensive to reveal the underlying classification logic and variations in accessibility. Fields of particular interest to me include campsite category, region, access method, dog policy, facilities and site capacity.

At this stage, I envisage the visualisation as an interactive piece displayed on a screen; it is likely to take the form of a web-based data visualisation, using maps or rearrangements to illustrate the relationships between different camps under various conditions. It will not merely be a neutral information tool, but will emphasise how environmental locations are translated into administrative and cultural data. I hope the audience will realise that public natural spaces are not simply places that ‘exist there, waiting to be visited’; they are also objects shaped by naming, classification and systems of governance. This approach relates to the concept of data humanism covered in the course, as I wish to preserve a sense of place, lived experience and context, rather than reducing these campsites entirely to abstract numbers. It also connects with data feminism, as it raises crucial questions: whose perspectives are prioritised within data systems, which assumptions are taken for granted as the norm, and what content is overlooked. Kirikowhai Mikaere’s discussion on Māori data sovereignty is also significant, as it reminds me to view environmental data as a strategic resource rather than merely neutral, publicly available information. I hope the impact of this work will be a shift in perspective: that audiences will no longer view environmental data as mere facts, but will begin to realise that it is, in fact, a designed system, and that this system is influencing how people in contemporary Aotearoa understand land, tenure and a sense of belonging.
